Relational Databases

Relational Databases are the great-grandmama of database management systems. First proposed in an ACM article published in the 70s by IBM computer scientist E.F. Codd, relational databases are the most common form of database used today. Although they come in a variety of flavors, they generally share the following characteristics:

Relational Database Characteristics

  • They are based on set theory mathematics and are implemented as two-dimensional tables
  • Relational databases use some form of Structured Query Language (SQL) for Creation, Reading, Updating, and Deletion
  • They consist of one or many tables that can be joined to create new tables on the fly

Popular Relational Database Implementations

There are a number of commercially-available implementations available. Some of the most popular open-source versions are:
  • PostgreSQL
  • MySQL
  • DuroDBMS
